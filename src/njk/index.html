Relating property assessments to property taxes

By Christian McDonald, Austin American-Statesman

There are a number of factors that affect a homeowner's property tax bill. While the buck stops with the taxing entities and the tax rates they set, your home value matters since that is what the tax rate is applied against. If a tax rate is the same from one year to the next, but your home value increases, then your tax bill increases, too.

It all starts with an "appraisal value," which is what your home (the land and your house) is worth on the market that year, as determined by the appraisal district. That appraisal is then used to determine the "assessed value" of a home, which takes into account the homestead exemption homeowners can have if the live in their home. The base exemption is set when a owner buys a house, and each year thereafter the assessed value cannot be raised higher than 10 percent of the previous year's value. This helps temper large increases in tax bills if a home value jumps a lot in a single year.

Finally, the "taxable value" is the "assessed value" minus any exemptions granted by the taxing entity, which can be different depending on the entity. The tax rate they set is applied against this "taxable value."

With so many variables at play, it is difficult to generalize how a tax bill will change year over year. We used appraisal district data to compare and contrast a few homes around Central Texas.
